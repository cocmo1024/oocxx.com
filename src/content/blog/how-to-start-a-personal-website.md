Starting a personal website is easier than it used to be, but it is also easier to make avoidable mistakes. Many people spend too long choosing tools, over-design the homepage, publish nothing, and then wonder why the site never turns into a real asset.

The better approach is simpler: decide what the site is for, publish the essential pages first, and set up a structure that can grow with your writing.

## Decide what the website must do

A personal website does not need to do everything.

It usually needs to do three things well:

- explain who you are
- show what you make, know, or write about
- give search engines and readers a clear path through the site

If you are still unsure about the shape of the site, start by reading [personal website vs portfolio](/blog/personal-website-vs-portfolio/). That decision affects your homepage, your navigation, and the kind of content you publish next.

## Publish the minimum set of pages first

The strongest first version of a personal website is usually small.

Start with:

- a homepage that explains what the site is about
- an about page that gives context and credibility
- a contact page that gives people a clear way to reach you
- one or two useful articles or project pages
- a simple navigation bar

That is enough to make the site legible.

If you need help deciding the actual page set, use [what pages should a personal website have](/blog/what-pages-should-a-personal-website-have/). If your homepage is vague, use the [personal website homepage checklist](/blog/personal-website-homepage-checklist/). If your about page is weak, use [this guide to writing an about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/).

## Pick a structure that can scale

A website that begins with only a homepage often stays thin forever. A website that begins with a structure can grow.

That structure should include:

- broad topics
- a consistent naming style for pages and posts
- a lightweight tag system
- internal links between related articles

You do not need dozens of categories. You need a few stable shelves.

That is why [blog categories vs tags](/blog/blog-categories-vs-tags/) matters early. It is easier to build a clean site structure now than to untangle a messy archive later.

## Choose tools, then stop thinking about tools

People lose months on platform anxiety.

Use a stack that lets you:

- publish quickly
- control your URLs
- edit titles and descriptions
- link pages together without friction
- keep the site fast on mobile

If your current stack already does those things, it is probably good enough. The difference between a growing site and a stagnant one is rarely the CMS. It is usually the quality of the pages and the consistency of the publishing.

## Write pages that solve one real problem at a time

A personal website becomes discoverable when its pages have clear intent.

Weak examples:

- thoughts
- notes
- projects
- my journey

Stronger examples:

- how to write an about page
- what to put on a contact page
- personal website homepage checklist
- internal linking for bloggers
- personal website SEO checklist

Specific pages attract better search traffic, are easier to interlink, and give readers a reason to stay.

## Build your first internal link loop

One article is a page. Three connected articles are the start of a system.

At minimum, your first articles should link to each other naturally:

- a homepage guide should link to an about page guide
- a structure guide should link to categories, tags, and navigation
- a growth guide should link to internal linking and SEO basics

That is the foundation of topical depth. It also helps visitors discover more than one page per visit.

If you want a deliberate version of that system, read [internal linking for bloggers](/blog/internal-linking-for-bloggers/).

## Make search friendliness part of the build, not a later repair

Search traffic is easier to earn when the site is organized correctly from the beginning.

That means:

- descriptive page titles
- clean URLs
- one clear topic per article
- strong internal links
- mobile-friendly layouts
- helpful summaries near the top of the page

You do not need advanced SEO before launch. You do need a site that makes sense. Use the [personal website SEO checklist](/blog/personal-website-seo-checklist/) as your baseline.

## What to do in the first week

If you want the shortest possible launch plan, do this:

1. Decide whether the site is mainly a personal website, a portfolio, or a hybrid.
2. Publish the homepage and about page.
3. Write two useful articles tied to one clear topic.
4. Set up categories, tags, and simple navigation.
5. Link every page to at least two related pages.

That is enough to launch something real.

The goal is not to make a perfect website. The goal is to publish a site that is understandable, useful, and able to grow.
