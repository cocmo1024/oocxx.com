Using your own name as a domain is often the cleanest answer.

It is also not the right answer for every site.

The real question is whether the website should grow around you, around a topic, or around a brand that might eventually be larger than one person.

## When your own name is a strong choice

Using your own name usually works best when:

- the site is a personal website
- you are a generalist
- your work may evolve over time
- the site will hold both writing and projects

This is why own-name domains are so common for writers, designers, developers, consultants, and researchers.

The name gives you room to change direction without changing the whole site identity.

## When your own name may not be the best choice

It may be weaker when:

- the site is built around a narrow niche publication
- the project needs to feel larger than one person
- the name itself is hard to spell or remember
- you already have a stronger descriptive brand

In those cases, a topic-led or brand-led domain may carry less friction.

## Why own-name domains age well

An own-name domain often holds up because the site can change while the identity stays stable.

You can move from:

- portfolio to blog
- blog to consultancy
- niche writing to broader publishing

without the domain suddenly feeling strange.

That flexibility is hard to beat.

## Why some people still regret it

People usually regret an own-name domain for one of three reasons:

- they wanted the site to feel more like a publication
- they dislike being the visible center of the brand
- their name is difficult to spell, type, or pronounce

Those are real reasons, not edge cases.

## Use your own name when the site is primarily about your body of work

This is especially strong for:

- portfolio plus blog hybrids
- personal websites for public-facing professionals
- long-term archives of work and writing

If the site is fundamentally about your evolving work, your name is often the most durable umbrella.

That is one reason it fits so well with the personal-website side of [personal website vs portfolio](/blog/personal-website-vs-portfolio/).

## Do not use your own name just because it feels safer

Sometimes people choose an own-name domain because they cannot decide anything else.

That can still work, but it should be a real choice.

If the site is obviously trying to become:

- a niche resource
- a publication
- a themed project

then a more descriptive domain may serve the reader better.

## A simple decision rule

Use your own name if:

- the site is meant to grow with you
- your identity is the main connective thread
- you want maximum flexibility

Choose something else if:

- the topic itself is the main product
- the project needs a distinct brand identity
- your name creates too much spelling friction

## The middle-ground option

Some people use:

- their own name for the main site
- a descriptive title for the publication or newsletter inside it

That is often a very good compromise.

It lets the domain stay durable while the editorial project gets a more specific label.
