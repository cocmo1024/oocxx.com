Choosing a domain name feels strangely permanent, which is why people overcomplicate it.

They chase originality, hide behind cryptic abbreviations, or spend weeks looking for a perfect name that does not exist.

The better standard is simpler: pick a domain that is clear enough to live with for years.

## What a good domain name needs to do

A strong domain name usually does four things:

- feels easy to remember
- is easy to spell
- does not box the site in too early
- still sounds reasonable when said out loud

That is enough.

You do not need a genius name. You need a workable one.

## Clarity beats cleverness

Most naming mistakes come from trying too hard to sound distinctive.

That often produces names that are:

- hard to spell
- hard to pronounce
- hard to trust
- hard to share verbally

If someone hears the domain once and cannot type it correctly, the name is already doing extra damage.

## Start with the role of the site

Before choosing a domain, ask what the site is actually trying to become.

For example:

- a personal website built around your own identity
- a niche publication
- a portfolio and blog hybrid
- a studio or solo business site

The right name depends on the job.

If you are still deciding the site shape itself, start with [how to start a personal website](/blog/how-to-start-a-personal-website/) and [personal website ideas](/blog/personal-website-ideas/).

## Personal-name domains are often the cleanest default

For many people, the best default is:

- `firstname lastname`
- `firstnamelastname`
- `firstname-initial-lastname`

This works especially well when:

- the site is about your work over time
- you are a generalist
- you may change focus later

It gives you the most flexibility.

If you are unsure about that tradeoff, read [should you use your own name as your domain](/blog/should-you-use-your-own-name-as-a-domain/).

## Descriptive domains can work too

A descriptive domain can be strong when the site is tightly focused.

This is more useful for:

- niche publications
- resource hubs
- topic-driven sites

The risk is that descriptive names can become limiting if the site grows beyond the original concept.

## Keep the structure simple

The safest names are usually:

- short
- readable
- not overloaded with punctuation

Try to avoid:

- unusual spellings
- doubled letters that are easy to miss
- long hyphenated phrases
- numbers unless they are truly part of the brand

Simple beats ornate almost every time.

## The extension matters less than the name, but it still matters

For most personal sites, `.com` is still the cleanest option when available.

If it is not available, that does not automatically block the project.

Good alternatives can still work, but the name itself should stay readable and trustworthy.

The mistake is not choosing a non-`.com` extension.

The mistake is choosing a confusing domain because the cleaner version was unavailable.

## Think about future flexibility

The domain should fit the site you want in a year, not just the site you can imagine tonight.

That means asking:

- will this still work if I publish more broadly?
- will it still make sense if the site includes a blog and portfolio?
- will I be embarrassed saying this out loud in a professional context?

Those questions rule out a lot of fragile names quickly.

## What to do if the perfect name is taken

Do not spiral.

Use one of these moves:

- choose your own name
- shorten the phrase
- widen the concept slightly
- pick a clearer alternative instead of forcing a clever variant

The right move is usually to improve the name, not to decorate it with extra characters.

## A good domain name supports trust, not rankings magic

The domain itself is not a shortcut to SEO.

What it does affect is:

- memorability
- click confidence
- brand clarity
- how natural the site feels when shared

That still matters a lot.

## The best rule

Choose the most readable domain that still fits the site you actually want to build.

That rule is not glamorous, but it leads to better names than most brainstorming sessions do.
