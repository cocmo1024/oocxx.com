People ask how long a blog post should be for SEO because they want a number.

What they usually need is a standard.

The useful standard is not a universal word count. It is whether the post answers the search intent clearly enough to deserve its place in the archive.

## The short answer

For many small websites, strong posts often fall somewhere between:

- 800 and 2,000 words

But that range only works as a rough reference.

The right length depends on:

- how complex the question is
- how much context the reader needs
- what the competing pages are trying to solve

Some posts should be shorter. Some should be longer.

## Why word count alone is a weak SEO metric

Longer posts can perform well, but not because search engines reward length by itself.

Longer posts often perform because they:

- answer more subquestions
- create better structure
- give more context
- attract more internal-linking opportunities

If the extra length does not improve the answer, it is just padding.

## What a post needs instead of an arbitrary length target

A post needs enough room to:

- define the problem clearly
- answer the main question
- cover the important objections or edge cases
- link to the next relevant pages

That is a better standard than trying to hit 1,500 words because someone on social media said that number ranks.

## When a post is too short

A post is often too short when:

- the intro says little
- key subquestions are missing
- the reader still has obvious unanswered questions
- the page feels more like notes than a finished answer

Thin pages often struggle not because they are short, but because they are incomplete.

## When a post is too long

A post is often too long when:

- the same point keeps repeating
- weak sections were added only to increase word count
- the structure makes scanning harder
- the page tries to answer three different questions at once

Bloated posts can perform worse because they reduce clarity.

## Match length to intent

Different intents call for different lengths.

### Simple utility question

A direct question such as:

- how many categories should a blog have

may only need a compact, focused answer.

### Mid-depth practical question

A question such as:

- how often should you blog for SEO

usually needs a fuller explanation with tradeoffs and examples.

### Broad strategic question

A question such as:

- how to start a personal website

may need a longer guide because it covers multiple decisions and pathways.

This is why intent matters more than a fixed word count.

## Structure often matters more than length

A clear 1,000-word post can outperform a messy 2,500-word post.

What usually matters most is:

- a strong title
- a sharp opening summary
- clean subheads
- enough detail in the right places

If structure is weak, adding more words rarely fixes the real problem.

## How to decide if a draft needs more length

Ask:

- have I actually answered the reader's core question?
- are the missing pieces important or merely interesting?
- would an internal link serve the reader better than another paragraph?

That last question matters.

Sometimes the best way to avoid bloat is to keep the page focused and link to a dedicated support guide.

## Use clusters to keep posts focused

One reason topic clusters work well is that they let each page stay specific.

Instead of forcing one article to cover everything, you can publish:

- one focused guide
- two or three supporting articles
- strong internal links between them

That is usually better for readers and for search.

See [internal linking for bloggers](/blog/internal-linking-for-bloggers/) for the practical system behind that.

## The better question

Do not ask only:

- how long should this post be?

Ask:

- how much does this question need?

That keeps the page aligned with search intent instead of vanity metrics.
