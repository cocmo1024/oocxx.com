The homepage headline carries more weight than most people realize.

If the first line is vague, the whole site feels vague.

That is why a strong headline usually beats a fancy hero layout.

## What a homepage headline needs to do

A good homepage headline should quickly tell the visitor:

- who the site is for
- what kind of work, knowledge, or writing lives here
- why they should keep reading

It does not need to be clever.

It needs to be legible.

If the page structure itself is weak, fix that first with the [personal website homepage checklist](/blog/personal-website-homepage-checklist/).

## A simple formula that works

For most personal websites, this formula is enough:

**Role or identity + what you do + what the site helps with**

Examples:

- Designer sharing practical systems for small websites.
- Writer publishing field notes on local architecture and cities.
- Consultant helping early-stage teams make clearer product decisions.

You can make the line shorter or more specific, but the logic stays the same.

## Headline examples for different kinds of personal websites

### 1. The writer site

- Essays and guides about independent websites, publishing, and search.
- Writer documenting how small sites become durable assets.
- Notes on writing, research, and building a readable web presence.

### 2. The designer site

- Designer building calm, useful interfaces for small teams.
- Product designer sharing systems, case notes, and practical web thinking.
- Design work and field notes on content, structure, and usability.

### 3. The developer site

- Developer writing about web architecture, tooling, and small-site systems.
- Building websites, documenting process, and publishing what works.
- Code, guides, and technical notes for durable web projects.

### 4. The consultant site

- Strategy, writing, and structure for companies that need clearer websites.
- Consultant helping small teams sharpen messaging and site architecture.
- Practical guidance on positioning, content systems, and growth pages.

### 5. The educator or researcher site

- Research, guides, and teaching notes on language, systems, and the web.
- Educator publishing practical lessons for curious beginners and working professionals.
- A library of explanations, readings, and field notes.

### 6. The creator site

- Writing, projects, and experiments from an independent creator.
- A personal website about making things, publishing regularly, and learning in public.
- Essays, projects, and working notes from a one-person studio.

### 7. The generalist site

- Work, writing, and experiments across product, publishing, and small business.
- A home for projects, essays, and systems thinking.
- Notes from a generalist building useful things on the web.

## When the headline should be direct

Be direct when:

- the site is professional
- the audience is new to you
- the homepage must create trust fast

Direct headlines often feel less glamorous while you write them, but they usually perform better because they reduce confusion.

## When the headline can be slightly looser

You have more room for texture when:

- the audience already knows you
- the site is editorial rather than service-led
- the supporting sentence does a lot of clarifying work

Even then, the reader still needs orientation.

If the headline is more atmospheric, the line beneath it should become more explicit. That is where [website tagline examples for personal websites](/blog/website-tagline-examples-for-personal-websites/) can help.

## Common headline mistakes

Avoid these:

- "Hi, I'm Alex"
- "Welcome to my corner of the internet"
- slogans that could describe almost anyone
- abstract phrases with no topic or role

Those lines may sound warm, but they do not explain the site.

## How to improve a weak headline

Ask:

- what is the site mainly about?
- what kind of person is behind it?
- what kind of visitor should feel at home here?

Then rewrite the line so a stranger can answer those questions in a few seconds.
