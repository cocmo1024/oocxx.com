A personal website homepage should not begin by trying to sound impressive.

It should create orientation.

When a stranger lands on the page, they need to understand four things quickly:

- who is behind the site;
- what the site is about;
- what kind of proof or writing exists here;
- where to go next.

Everything else is secondary.

## The first sentence has one job

The first sentence should remove confusion.

Weak:

> Welcome to my corner of the internet.

Better:

> I write practical guides for independent designers building smaller, clearer websites.

The second version tells the visitor what the site is about, who it may help, and what kind of content to expect.

It does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be useful.

For more headline patterns, use [homepage headline examples for personal websites](/blog/homepage-headline-examples-for-personal-websites/). If you need the shorter supporting line, use [website tagline examples for personal websites](/blog/website-tagline-examples-for-personal-websites/).

## A simple homepage copy formula

Use this structure:

1. `Identity`: who you are or what the site is.
2. `Focus`: what subject, work, or problem the site covers.
3. `Value`: what a reader can learn, use, hire, or explore.
4. `Next step`: where the reader should go first.

Example:

> I'm Maya Chen, a product designer writing about accessible interface patterns, portfolio case studies, and practical design systems for small teams. Start with the field notes or browse selected projects.

That paragraph is not fancy. It is clear.

## What the homepage should say above the fold

The first screen should usually include:

- a specific headline;
- a one- or two-sentence explanation;
- a few clear navigation labels;
- one primary next step;
- optional proof if it fits naturally.

Do not force the visitor to scroll through a giant visual section before learning what the site is. Design can create atmosphere, but the words still have to do the orientation work.

This is why the [personal website homepage checklist](/blog/personal-website-homepage-checklist/) starts with understanding rather than decoration.

## Say what kind of site it is

A personal website can be many things:

- a portfolio;
- a blog;
- a professional profile;
- a project archive;
- a learning journal;
- a resource library;
- a hybrid of several formats.

The homepage should make the dominant shape obvious.

If the site is a portfolio with writing, say that. If it is mainly a blog, say what the blog covers. If it is a resource library, explain the topic and who should use it.

Readers should not need five clicks to understand the site category.

## Add proof without overloading the page

Proof can be:

- selected projects;
- recent articles;
- featured guides;
- clients or publications;
- a short credibility line;
- a clear explanation of your experience.

The mistake is trying to show all proof at once.

A homepage should show enough proof to create trust and then route the reader deeper. If you have many projects, link to a dedicated work page. If you have many articles, link to a blog or topic index. If your background needs explanation, link to the about page.

Use [how to write an about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/) when the homepage summary is becoming too long.

## Make the next step explicit

The homepage should not end with a dead zone.

Good next steps include:

- Read the latest guide;
- Browse the project archive;
- Start with the beginner guide;
- View selected work;
- Contact me;
- Read the about page.

The right next step depends on the site purpose. A consultant may want a contact route. A writer may want a reading path. A maker may want a project page.

If your homepage has more than three competing calls to action, the page probably needs stronger priority.

## Wording mistakes that weaken homepages

Common mistakes:

- saying "I do many things" without naming the useful ones;
- using creative labels before the visitor understands the site;
- leading with personality instead of context;
- writing a long biography above the fold;
- using a slogan that could describe thousands of people;
- hiding the blog, work, or contact path.

These mistakes do not make the site more personal. They make it harder to use.

## Examples of stronger homepage copy

For a writer:

> I publish essays and practical notes about independent publishing, personal websites, and building a small body of work online.

For a developer:

> I'm a frontend developer documenting small interface systems, Astro sites, accessibility fixes, and the tradeoffs behind real implementation work.

For a designer:

> I design calm web interfaces for small teams and write about homepage structure, product clarity, and design systems that do not collapse under maintenance.

For a researcher:

> This site collects research notes, reading maps, and plain-language explainers about urban technology and public-interest data.

Each version gives the visitor a usable frame.

## If you do many things

Do not list everything in the first sentence.

Choose the strongest umbrella.

Instead of:

> Designer, writer, developer, photographer, runner, teacher, and occasional consultant.

Try:

> I build and write about small digital products, with notes on design, code, and independent publishing.

The second version still leaves room for multiple interests, but it gives them a shared structure.

If this is your main problem, read [personal website ideas](/blog/personal-website-ideas/) and choose a site direction before rewriting the homepage.

## The ten-second test

After writing the homepage, show it to someone who does not know the project.

Ask:

- What is this site about?
- Who is it for?
- What should I click next?
- What seems most credible here?

If they cannot answer, the homepage needs clearer words before it needs more design.
