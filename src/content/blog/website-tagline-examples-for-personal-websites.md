A homepage tagline is usually the sentence that sits under the main headline.

Its job is not to sound profound.

Its job is to make the homepage easier to understand.

## Headline first, tagline second

The headline usually gives the strongest label.

The tagline adds context.

For example:

- headline: Designer building useful websites for small teams.
- tagline: Case studies, writing, and practical notes on content systems, navigation, and readable interfaces.

That pairing works because the headline identifies the person and the tagline explains what the site contains.

If your main headline is still weak, start with [homepage headline examples for personal websites](/blog/homepage-headline-examples-for-personal-websites/).

## What a good tagline should add

A strong tagline usually adds one or more of these:

- what the site covers
- what kind of material the visitor will find
- what kinds of problems the site helps solve

It should usually stay short enough to scan quickly.

## Tagline examples for different site types

### Writer or essay site

- Essays, guides, and working notes on publishing, research, and independent websites.
- Long-form writing about cities, language, and how people make sense of systems.
- Practical essays for people building a thoughtful online presence.

### Designer or developer site

- Selected work, technical notes, and small-system thinking from recent projects.
- Case studies, field notes, and experiments in structure, usability, and front-end craft.
- Building useful interfaces and documenting what holds up over time.

### Consultant or strategist site

- Guidance on messaging, structure, and content systems for small teams.
- Writing and client-facing work on positioning, site clarity, and growth pages.
- Practical thinking for founders who want clearer websites and better decisions.

### Educator or teacher site

- Guides, explainers, and reading notes for learners who want a clearer path into the subject.
- Lessons, references, and writing that make complicated topics easier to use.
- Teaching materials and practical essays for curious beginners.

### Generalist personal site

- Projects, essays, and ongoing notes across product, publishing, and small business.
- A home for work, writing, and useful side trails.
- Selected projects, current interests, and durable things worth reading.

## What taglines should usually avoid

Avoid taglines that are:

- only emotional
- packed with too many topic lists
- more confusing than the headline itself

A tagline should clarify the page, not introduce new ambiguity.

## A simple tagline formula

For most sites, this formula works:

**What lives here + what kind of value it offers**

Examples:

- Guides, templates, and notes for building a readable personal website.
- Essays, projects, and practical ideas for working more clearly on the web.

That is often enough.

## Use the headline and tagline as a pair

The homepage gets stronger when the two lines work together:

- headline = identity or promise
- tagline = scope or proof

Do not ask one line to do everything.
