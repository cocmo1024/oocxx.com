LinkedIn and a personal website solve different problems.

LinkedIn is a professional network profile. It is useful because people already know how to search, verify, and contact you there.

A personal website is your controlled home base. It lets you decide the structure, the proof, the writing, the navigation, and the long-term archive.

The question is not usually "LinkedIn or personal website?"

The better answer is:

**Use LinkedIn for network context. Use your personal website for depth, proof, and ownership.**

## What LinkedIn is good at

LinkedIn is useful for:

- identity verification;
- employment history;
- social proof;
- mutual connections;
- recruiter discovery;
- quick contact;
- lightweight updates.

It is especially useful during a job search because recruiters and hiring teams already use it.

But LinkedIn is not built to explain your work in the exact structure you want.

## What a personal website is good at

A personal website is useful for:

- presenting selected work;
- shaping your homepage message;
- publishing longer writing;
- creating topic clusters;
- showing a portfolio;
- explaining your process;
- owning your domain and URLs;
- linking resume, contact, projects, and writing together.

It gives you more control than a social profile.

If the site is specifically for job search, read [personal website for job search](/blog/personal-website-for-job-search/).

## The ownership difference

LinkedIn is rented space.

Your personal website is owned space.

That does not mean you should ignore LinkedIn. It means you should not make it the only place where your professional identity lives.

Platforms change layouts, algorithms, visibility, and norms. A personal website changes when you decide to change it.

## The structure difference

LinkedIn has a fixed structure:

- headline;
- experience;
- education;
- skills;
- posts;
- recommendations;
- profile sections.

A personal website can have a structure that fits the actual goal:

- homepage;
- about;
- work;
- resume;
- case studies;
- writing;
- contact;
- topic pages.

That flexibility matters when you need to show work in a way a profile cannot.

## The search difference

LinkedIn can rank for your name and professional identity.

A personal website can also rank for:

- your name;
- your role;
- your projects;
- your writing topics;
- guides or resources you publish;
- niche expertise connected to your work.

That broader search surface is one reason a personal website can become more valuable over time.

If the site is live but hard to find, use [how to get a personal website on Google](/blog/how-to-get-a-personal-website-on-google/).

## When LinkedIn may be enough

LinkedIn may be enough when:

- you need a simple professional profile;
- you are not publishing work samples;
- your field evaluates mostly through credentials;
- you do not want to maintain another site;
- your main goal is network presence, not publishing or portfolio depth.

Even then, a minimal personal website can still be useful as a canonical contact and proof page.

## When a personal website matters more

A personal website matters more when:

- you have projects to show;
- you write or teach;
- your work needs explanation;
- you want search visibility beyond your name;
- you want a portfolio and blog hybrid;
- you want a long-term archive that is not tied to one platform.

This is especially true for designers, developers, writers, researchers, consultants, independent makers, and career changers.

## How to use both together

Use LinkedIn to point to the personal website.

Use the personal website to give depth that LinkedIn cannot.

A simple setup:

1. LinkedIn headline matches the website positioning.
2. LinkedIn featured link points to the website or best work page.
3. Website homepage links back to LinkedIn if useful.
4. Website about page gives more context than the LinkedIn summary.
5. Website work page shows selected proof.
6. Website contact page explains the best way to reach you.

The two should reinforce each other, not contradict each other.

## What to avoid

Avoid:

- making the website a duplicate of LinkedIn;
- hiding the website link on LinkedIn;
- writing different positioning in each place;
- publishing a personal website with no proof;
- using LinkedIn posts as the only archive of your thinking.

Consistency matters. If a recruiter moves from LinkedIn to your website, the story should become clearer, not more confusing.

## The practical rule

Use LinkedIn for reach and verification.

Use your personal website for control, proof, and depth.

If you care about long-term discoverability, trust, or a body of work, the personal website should become the professional home base.
