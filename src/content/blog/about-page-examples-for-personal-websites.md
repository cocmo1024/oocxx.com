Most about pages fail because they are too vague to create trust and too self-focused to guide the reader.

That is why examples help.

A good about page example is not valuable because it sounds clever. It is valuable because it shows what the page is doing.

## What a good about page example includes

A strong about page usually contains:

- a clear summary of who you are
- context for what the site covers
- a reason to trust you
- a path to something useful next

If you still need the structure behind the examples, read [how to write an about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/).

## Example 1: The simple expert introduction

This works for:

- consultants
- specialists
- people with a clear domain

Example:

I am a freelance strategist writing about small websites, homepage structure, and content systems that help independent sites grow through search.

On this site I publish practical guides for people building a website that needs to feel clear, trustworthy, and easy to expand.

Why it works:

- it says what the person does
- it says what the site covers
- it gives the visitor a reason to keep reading

## Example 2: The writer-focused about page

This works for:

- bloggers
- essayists
- researchers

Example:

I write about how small sites become useful over time: not through constant publishing, but through structure, archives, and pages that keep solving real problems.

This site collects guides, notes, and practical frameworks for people who want a quieter, more durable web presence.

Why it works:

- it leads with the writing
- it defines the editorial lens
- it positions the site without sounding corporate

## Example 3: The hybrid work-and-writing introduction

This works for:

- designers
- developers
- product people

Example:

I design and build digital products. This website does two things: it shows selected work, and it publishes the systems I use to think through structure, content, and product clarity.

If you are here for proof, start with the work. If you are here for ideas, start with the guides.

Why it works:

- it respects two different visitor intents
- it links the site sections together
- it makes the homepage and about page tell the same story

## Example 4: The personal-but-useful introduction

This works for:

- generalists
- independent creators
- people with a warmer tone

Example:

I am interested in small systems that compound: websites, notes, writing habits, and structures that keep getting more useful instead of more noisy.

This site is where I organize those interests into articles, examples, and practical references.

Why it works:

- it has personality
- it still explains the purpose of the site
- it avoids empty slogan language

## Example 5: The credibility paragraph

Many about pages need one short proof section.

Example:

Over the past decade I have worked across content, design, and site strategy for small teams and independent projects. The patterns on this site come from that work: what actually helps people understand a website faster and what keeps a small archive useful over time.

Why it works:

- it adds trust without reading like a resume dump
- it keeps the proof relevant to the site topic
- it moves naturally into the guides

## What weak about page examples tend to do

Weak about pages often sound like this:

- "I am passionate about innovation and storytelling."
- "This is my corner of the internet."
- "I wear many hats."

These lines are not false. They are just not useful.

They do not tell the visitor enough to understand the site or trust the person behind it.

## How to adapt these examples for your site

Take the structure, not the exact wording.

Make sure your version answers:

- what you do
- what the site covers
- what kind of visitor should keep reading
- where they should go next

That is why the about page is closely tied to the homepage, navigation, and contact page. If those pieces are weak, the about page feels weaker too.

## Add one clear next step

A good about page should point people somewhere.

Usually that means linking to:

- the best guide on the site
- the main writing archive
- the work page
- the contact page

If you want a trust path that stays clean, pair this page with a dedicated [contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/) rather than overloading the about page itself.
