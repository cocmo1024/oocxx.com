A contact page does not need much copy, but the copy it does have matters.

The right example can help you avoid the two most common failures:

- a contact page that says almost nothing
- a contact page that tries to do too much

## What a strong contact page example shows

A useful contact page example makes three things clear:

- how to get in touch
- what the site owner wants to hear about
- what happens next

If you need the underlying structure first, use [what to put on a contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/).

## Example 1: The simplest email-first contact page

This works for:

- personal sites
- writers
- portfolio sites

Example:

If you want to get in touch about my writing, a project, or a thoughtful question related to the site, email me at `hello@example.com`.

I usually reply within a few business days.

Why it works:

- it is direct
- it tells people what the email is for
- it gives a response expectation

## Example 2: The project inquiry version

This works for:

- freelancers
- consultants
- service businesses

Example:

For project inquiries, email `hello@example.com` with a short note about your timeline, budget, and what kind of help you need.

If the project is a good fit, I will reply with next steps.

Why it works:

- it filters vague messages
- it sets expectations early
- it keeps the page practical instead of overexplained

## Example 3: The creator-friendly version

This works for:

- creators
- independent bloggers
- public-facing personal websites

Example:

You can reach me at `hello@example.com` for thoughtful replies, collaboration ideas, and questions about the site.

If you only want to browse first, the [guides](/blog/) and [about page](/about/) are better places to start.

Why it works:

- it welcomes human contact without sounding open-ended
- it routes casual visitors elsewhere
- it keeps the contact page from becoming the only orientation page

## Example 4: The limited-availability version

This works for:

- busy solo operators
- people who need boundaries

Example:

I read every message, but I cannot reply to everything. Email is best for project inquiries, interviews, and site-related questions.

For general browsing, start with the [homepage](/) or [topic pages](/topics/).

Why it works:

- it sets realistic expectations
- it protects time without sounding rude
- it still gives the visitor a next step

## Example 5: The form-plus-email version

This works for:

- people who use a simple form
- sites with a lot of inquiry volume

Example:

Use the form below for project inquiries. If you prefer email, write to `hello@example.com`.

I usually respond within three business days.

Why it works:

- it makes the preferred path clear
- it keeps the form from feeling like a trap
- it still gives a fallback option

## What weak contact page examples get wrong

Weak contact pages often sound like this:

- "Feel free to reach out anytime!"
- "Let's connect."
- "Drop me a line."

The problem is not that these phrases are bad on their own.

The problem is that they do not say enough.

The visitor still does not know:

- what the message should be about
- how you prefer to be contacted
- whether they should expect a reply

## Keep the tone aligned with the site

The contact page should sound like the rest of the website.

If the site is calm and editorial, the contact page should not suddenly sound like startup sales copy.

If the site is warm and personal, the contact page should not read like a legal notice.

That is why these examples are best used as patterns, not scripts.

## Pair the contact page with better site structure

A contact page works better when the site already has:

- a clear homepage
- a useful about page
- a visible writing or work section

Without those pages, too much context gets forced onto the contact page itself. If your structure still feels shaky, use [what pages should a personal website have](/blog/what-pages-should-a-personal-website-have/) first.
