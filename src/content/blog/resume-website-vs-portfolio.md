A resume website and a portfolio are not the same thing.

They can live on the same personal website, but they answer different questions.

A resume website asks:

**What is this person's background?**

A portfolio asks:

**What can this person actually do?**

For job search, hiring, freelance work, and referrals, the strongest structure usually combines both.

## What a resume website is

A resume website is an online version of your professional summary.

It usually includes:

- role or target role;
- experience;
- education;
- skills;
- credentials;
- selected achievements;
- contact information;
- a downloadable resume or CV.

This format is useful when someone needs a fast overview.

It is less useful when the reader needs to evaluate real work quality.

## What a portfolio is

A portfolio is a proof system.

It usually includes:

- selected projects;
- case studies;
- screenshots or artifacts;
- your role;
- constraints;
- decisions;
- outcomes;
- links to live work if available.

A portfolio should not be a full archive. It should be selective.

For a broader site-shape comparison, read [personal website vs portfolio](/blog/personal-website-vs-portfolio/).

## The main difference

The difference is simple:

| Format | Main job |
| --- | --- |
| Resume website | Summarize credentials |
| Portfolio | Prove capability |
| Personal website | Connect identity, proof, writing, and contact |

That is why the best answer is often not "resume website or portfolio."

It is a personal website with a resume route and a portfolio route.

## When a resume website is enough

A resume website may be enough when:

- the role is credential-heavy;
- the reader mainly needs work history;
- project samples are not available or not allowed;
- you need a clean professional profile;
- the site is a supplement to LinkedIn and applications.

Even then, include enough context to make the page feel human. A web page that only repeats a resume can feel thin.

## When a portfolio is more important

A portfolio matters more when:

- you are a designer, developer, writer, marketer, researcher, photographer, or consultant;
- work quality is judged through examples;
- process and judgment matter;
- projects can be shown publicly;
- you need to prove a specific type of capability.

For these roles, a resume alone often cannot carry enough proof.

## When to combine both

Combine both when the site supports job search, freelance work, or professional discovery.

A strong combined structure:

- homepage: role, positioning, and best next steps;
- about: credibility and current direction;
- resume: concise credentials or download;
- work: selected portfolio items;
- writing: optional proof of thinking;
- contact: clear route for opportunities.

This structure lets different visitors choose the path they need.

Recruiters may scan the resume. Hiring managers may inspect the work. Referrals may read the about page. Search visitors may land on writing and then follow links to proof.

## What a resume page should include

A resume page should be concise.

Include:

- current role or target role;
- short summary;
- selected experience;
- relevant skills;
- credentials or education if useful;
- downloadable PDF if appropriate;
- contact and LinkedIn links.

Do not paste a giant resume with no hierarchy. The page should be easier to scan than a document.

## What a portfolio page should include

A portfolio page should help the reader evaluate your work.

For each selected project, include:

- project name;
- context;
- your role;
- problem;
- contribution;
- outcome or lesson;
- relevant artifacts.

The most common weak portfolio mistake is showing final visuals or links without explaining your role and decisions.

## What not to do

Avoid:

- using a resume page as the whole website;
- listing every job without showing proof;
- publishing too many weak projects;
- hiding contact information;
- making the portfolio impossible to scan;
- mixing personal notes, resume history, and project proof without structure.

If the site is for job search, clarity beats cleverness.

## A practical decision rule

Use this:

- If the reader needs credentials, build a resume page.
- If the reader needs proof of work, build a portfolio.
- If the reader needs both, build a personal website that contains both.

Most serious job-search websites need the third option.
