The about page is one of the most visited pages on a personal website, and one of the easiest to get wrong.

Many about pages are either too thin or too self-absorbed. They read like placeholder biography text, and they do not help the visitor understand why the site exists or why the person behind it is worth following.

## What an about page is supposed to do

An about page is not only biography.

It should help the reader answer:

- Who is behind this site?
- What is this site about?
- Why should I trust the person writing it?
- What should I read or do next?

That means the page should combine identity, context, and direction.

## A simple structure that works

Most personal website about pages can follow this order:

1. A short summary of who you are
2. A paragraph explaining what the site covers
3. Relevant proof, background, or experience
4. A short section on current work or interests
5. Links to important pages

This works because it moves from introduction to credibility to action.

## Lead with a useful summary

The top of the page should not start with your entire life story.

Start with two or three sentences that tell the reader:

- what you do
- what you write about
- what kind of website this is

Example structure:

I'm a designer writing about small websites, editorial systems, and how independent sites grow through search.

That is clearer than a paragraph of personality traits.

## Add proof without trying to impress everyone

Readers do not need your full resume. They need the right reasons to trust you.

That might include:

- years of experience
- notable work
- companies or clients
- the kinds of problems you have solved

Be selective. Credibility is stronger when it is relevant.

## Explain why the site exists

This is the part many about pages miss.

Do not only describe yourself. Describe the purpose of the site.

For example:

- why you publish here instead of only on social media
- who the content is for
- what kinds of problems the site tries to solve

This turns the page from biography into orientation.

## Link outward from the about page

The about page should not be a dead end.

It should point toward:

- your homepage
- your best article or guide
- your work or portfolio section
- a contact page if relevant

This is especially important on sites that want search traffic. The about page often becomes a trust page for first-time visitors who land on an article and then look for context.

If the site does not yet have a dedicated contact route, build one. A clear [contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/) makes the site feel much more complete.

## Common about page mistakes

Avoid these:

- writing in slogans instead of specifics
- listing every job you have ever had
- making the page only about personality
- hiding what the site is actually for
- ending with no path to another page

The page should feel warm, but it should still do a job.

## How long should an about page be?

Long enough to create confidence. Short enough to stay readable.

For most people, that means:

- 300 to 800 words
- clear subheads
- short paragraphs
- a few links to important next pages

If you need more than that, consider linking to a separate resume, CV, or timeline.

## Write it for a first-time visitor

The best way to improve your about page is to imagine the reader has never heard of you.

They are asking:

- Why this person?
- Why this site?
- What should I read next?

Answer those well, and the page works.
