A personal website can help a job search, but only when it is built for fast evaluation.

Recruiters, hiring managers, collaborators, and referrals are usually not reading your site like a long essay. They are trying to answer a few practical questions quickly:

- What kind of role does this person fit?
- What have they done?
- Is there credible proof?
- Can I see the best work without hunting?
- Is it easy to contact or verify them?

That means a job-search personal website needs more discipline than a casual personal archive.

## What the site should do

A job-search personal website should support three jobs:

1. `Positioning`: explain the kind of work you do and the role you want.
2. `Proof`: show selected projects, writing, case studies, or outcomes.
3. `Routing`: send the visitor to resume, work, contact, LinkedIn, or relevant writing quickly.

It does not need to show everything. It needs to show the right things.

If you are still choosing the site shape, read [personal website vs portfolio](/blog/personal-website-vs-portfolio/) first. A job-search site is usually a personal website with a strong portfolio or proof section.

## The homepage should be direct

The homepage should not open with a vague welcome line.

For job search, the first screen should usually include:

- your name;
- your role or target role;
- a short positioning sentence;
- one or two proof points;
- links to work, resume, about, and contact.

Weak:

> Welcome to my digital home.

Stronger:

> Product designer focused on onboarding, content systems, and accessible interface patterns for early-stage SaaS teams.

The second version gives a hiring reader useful context immediately.

Use [what should a personal website homepage say](/blog/what-should-a-personal-website-homepage-say/) if the top of the page is still vague.

## What pages a job-search website needs

The minimum useful structure is:

- `Home`: role, positioning, key proof, next steps.
- `About`: background, credibility, current direction.
- `Work` or `Projects`: selected proof, not every project.
- `Resume` or `CV`: downloadable or linked if appropriate.
- `Contact`: clear route for relevant opportunities.
- `Writing` or `Notes`: optional, useful when it proves judgment.

Do not add a blog just because the template includes one. Add writing only if it helps show your thinking, expertise, or communication.

For the general page-set decision, use [what pages should a personal website have](/blog/what-pages-should-a-personal-website-have/).

## Resume, portfolio, and personal website are different

A resume is a compact credential document.

A portfolio is selected proof of work.

A personal website is the container that can connect identity, proof, writing, contact, and context.

For many job searches, the best setup is:

- resume for application systems;
- LinkedIn for verification and network context;
- portfolio or work page for selected proof;
- personal website as the canonical home base.

If you are unsure how the pieces differ, read [resume website vs portfolio](/blog/resume-website-vs-portfolio/) and [LinkedIn vs personal website](/blog/linkedin-vs-personal-website/).

## What proof to show

Proof depends on the role.

For designers:

- case studies;
- before/after decisions;
- product screenshots;
- constraints and tradeoffs;
- measurable outcomes if available.

For developers:

- selected projects;
- technical writeups;
- GitHub or demo links;
- architecture notes;
- clear explanation of your role.

For writers or strategists:

- published samples;
- content systems;
- essays;
- client-facing work if permitted;
- examples of decision quality.

For students or career changers:

- coursework that resembles real work;
- self-directed projects;
- process notes;
- learning logs;
- evidence of consistency.

The important point is selection. A job-search site should not become a dump of everything you have ever done.

## The about page should support hiring

Your about page should not repeat the resume line by line.

It should explain:

- the kind of work you do;
- the kinds of problems you are good at;
- the background that makes you credible;
- what you are currently looking for;
- where the reader should go next.

Use [how to write an about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/) if the page is either too thin or too autobiographical.

## Make contact low-friction

A job-search site should make contact obvious.

Good contact paths include:

- email;
- LinkedIn;
- a contact form;
- a resume download;
- calendar link only if appropriate.

Also clarify what kinds of messages are welcome. If you are open to full-time roles, freelance work, internships, or collaborations, say so.

Use [what to put on a contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/) if the page is currently just an email address.

## What to avoid

Avoid:

- vague homepage copy;
- too many unfinished sections;
- project pages with no explanation of your role;
- a resume download with no surrounding context;
- a blog full of unrelated personal updates;
- hiding contact information;
- making every job you ever had equally prominent.

The site should help a hiring reader decide whether to continue.

## A simple job-search website structure

Use this:

1. Homepage with role, proof, and routing.
2. About page with current direction and credibility.
3. Work or projects page with 3 to 5 strong examples.
4. Resume or CV link.
5. Contact page with clear availability.
6. Optional writing section if it supports the role.

That is enough for most job-search personal websites.

## The standard

A personal website is useful for job search when it makes you easier to understand and easier to trust.

It does not need to be elaborate. It needs to reduce uncertainty for the people evaluating you.
