Navigation is one of the fastest ways to make a personal website feel clear or confusing.

When the menu is weak, the whole site feels weak. Visitors do not know where to go, search engines struggle to understand the structure, and good pages get buried.

## The main goal of navigation

Navigation should answer one question:

What are the main things this site wants me to explore?

That means the top-level menu is not a place to show every possible page. It is a place to show the main routes.

## Most small sites need fewer links than they think

For a personal website or editorial site, the top navigation is often strongest when it has four to six items.

A practical default:

- Home
- About
- Work or Projects
- Blog or Guides
- Contact

If you need more detail, use topic pages, archives, and internal links within the content.

## Use labels people understand immediately

Navigation labels should be obvious.

Good:

- About
- Writing
- Guides
- Projects

Risky:

- Playground
- Dispatches
- Field Notes
- Lab

Creative labels may feel distinctive, but they usually reduce clarity. A visitor should not have to decode your menu before they can browse.

## Keep the hierarchy shallow

The deeper the hierarchy, the weaker the site often feels.

For a small site:

- top-level navigation should be simple
- category pages should do the grouping
- individual posts should handle the depth

If every page lives behind multiple dropdowns, the structure becomes harder to crawl and harder to use.

## Navigation and homepage structure should match

Your homepage and your menu should tell the same story.

If the homepage says the site is about personal websites, but the navigation emphasizes unrelated buckets, the visitor gets mixed signals.

That is why [the homepage checklist](/blog/personal-website-homepage-checklist/) matters. The homepage and menu must reinforce each other.

## Add archive-style pathways for readers who want more

Not every reader wants to browse through the main menu.

Useful secondary pathways include:

- topic pages
- tag pages
- recent posts
- related guides

These are especially helpful on content-driven sites. They give readers multiple ways to continue without cluttering the primary navigation.

## Common navigation mistakes

The most common mistakes are:

- too many top-level links
- vague labels
- menus that change meaning from page to page
- dropdowns used to hide a messy structure
- no path from articles back to category or topic pages

Navigation problems are usually information architecture problems wearing a visual disguise.

## Build navigation around the site you want in a year

Do not design the menu only for what exists today.

Design it for the site you want after twenty or fifty posts.

That means:

- stable section names
- broad categories
- room for topic hubs
- predictable URLs

This connects directly to [blog categories vs tags](/blog/blog-categories-vs-tags/) and [internal linking for bloggers](/blog/internal-linking-for-bloggers/). Good navigation is not isolated. It is part of the whole publishing system.
