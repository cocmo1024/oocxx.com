For a certain kind of early website, opening the page did not just reveal text and images. It also triggered music.

Often that music was a looping MIDI track: tinny, compressed, instantly recognizable, and impossible to confuse with a silent modern interface.

## Why site owners added background music

They were trying to make a webpage feel like an environment.

Music could:

- set the tone of a fan site or personal homepage
- reinforce the emotional identity of the page
- make a site feel theatrical or immersive
- signal taste, mood, or affiliation

It was not subtle, but subtlety was rarely the point.

## Why MIDI fit the early web so well

MIDI was lightweight relative to other audio formats and easier to deploy in an era of slower connections. That made it practical as a repeating background layer for personal sites, especially those built around fandom, diaries, or mood-heavy design.

Like [blink and marquee text](/blog/blink-marquee-tags/), MIDI background music belonged to a web culture that wanted pages to announce themselves forcefully.

## Why it later became a punchline

Background music often started without warning. It could interrupt the visitor’s environment, clash with open tabs, and make the page feel intrusive rather than welcoming.

As web design shifted toward readability, speed, and user control, auto-playing audio became much harder to defend.

## Why it is still worth documenting

MIDI background music helps explain an old ambition of web design: the desire to make the page feel inhabited by more than text and image alone.

It turns the website into a staged atmosphere. That idea still survives in contemporary design, only now it is usually handled with more restraint.
