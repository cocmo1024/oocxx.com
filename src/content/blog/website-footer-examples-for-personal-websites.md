The footer is not the most glamorous part of a website, but it does quiet work.

It helps visitors orient themselves, find secondary links, and confirm that the site feels complete.

On a small site, that matters more than people think.

## What a footer needs to do

A good footer usually helps with three jobs:

- offer a final navigation path
- reinforce trust
- make the site easier to continue exploring

It does not need to be crowded.

It needs to be useful.

## What most personal website footers should include

Most small sites only need:

- site name or short description
- a few important links
- contact route
- copyright or ownership line if you want one

Optional additions:

- newsletter link
- social links
- now page
- topic index

If your contact setup is still unclear, use [what to put on a contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/) first.

## Footer example 1: The simple writer footer

This works for:

- blogs
- editorial sites
- essay archives

Example structure:

- site name
- one-line description
- links: About, Writing, Contact

Why it works:

- light and readable
- enough context for first-time visitors
- gives late-page readers an easy next step

## Footer example 2: The topic-and-archive footer

This works for:

- guide-heavy sites
- knowledge bases
- growing blogs

Example structure:

- short site description
- links: Start Here, Topics, Tags, Latest Posts, About, Contact

Why it works:

- creates more browsing paths without cluttering the top navigation
- supports deeper reading
- helps a content-heavy site feel organized

## Footer example 3: The consultant footer

This works for:

- solo consultants
- service businesses
- advisory sites

Example structure:

- one-line positioning statement
- links: Services, About, Guides, Contact
- short availability note if relevant

Why it works:

- reinforces who the site helps
- keeps inquiry routes visible
- still allows editorial pages to support trust

## Footer example 4: The creator footer

This works for:

- independent creators
- one-person brands
- hybrid work-and-writing sites

Example structure:

- site name
- links: Projects, Writing, About, Contact
- optional social or newsletter link

Why it works:

- compact
- personal without being messy
- makes cross-channel identity easier to understand

## Footer example 5: The minimal portfolio footer

This works for:

- compact portfolio sites
- visual work sites
- simple professional homepages

Example structure:

- name
- role
- links: Work, About, Contact

Why it works:

- mirrors the main navigation
- keeps the bottom of the page clean
- still closes the loop for visitors who scroll all the way down

## What not to bury in the footer

Do not hide important pages in the footer if they deserve stronger visibility.

That includes:

- your main writing archive
- your primary contact route
- the most important conversion page

The footer can support discovery, but it should not compensate for a weak header or homepage.

## How the footer fits the whole site

The best footer is aligned with the rest of the structure.

If the top navigation is spare, the footer can carry secondary paths.

If the site is content-heavy, the footer can help route people into topics and archives.

If the site is service-led, the footer can reinforce the main trust pages.
