Many early websites did not begin with a content-rich homepage. They began with a **splash page**: a short entrance screen that might contain a title image, a warning, a language choice, or a button that said “Enter.”

It sounds inefficient now, but it made sense within a very different culture of web publishing.

## What a splash page was meant to do

A splash page framed the website as an experience rather than a tool. It could:

- establish the mood of the site
- offer a visual identity before any navigation
- separate audiences by language or browser support
- delay the visitor long enough to make the site feel ceremonial

In other words, it turned entry into a small event.

## Why splash pages spread

The personal web often borrowed from posters, flyers, club scenes, and fan culture. Many site owners wanted the first click to feel dramatic. A splash page was a way to stage that feeling.

It also fit neatly with a web culture that did not yet worship speed above everything else. Visitors were expected to wait for images, sounds, and layout experiments. A short doorway did not feel outrageous in that context.

## Why splash pages became unpopular

They added friction. Over time, usability conventions shifted in favor of faster access, clearer hierarchy, and fewer unnecessary steps between the visitor and the information they wanted.

That shift made splash pages look indulgent, even when they were visually memorable.

## Why they still matter historically

The splash page shows that early websites often treated arrival as part of authorship. The page was saying: this is not just information; this is a place, and you are entering it.

That logic still echoes in modern landing pages and opening screens, even if the execution has become much more restrained.
