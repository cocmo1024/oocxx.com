Short author bios are deceptively hard.

Most people either write something too generic or overstuff a few lines with every credential they have ever earned.

A good short bio should feel clear, credible, and easy to place on:

- an about page
- a blog sidebar
- a guide archive
- a footer or profile block

## What a short author bio needs to do

A short bio should usually answer:

- who you are
- what you do or write about
- why the reader should trust you

That is it.

If you need the full version, that belongs on an [about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/). The short bio is for fast orientation.

## A simple short bio formula

The simplest formula is:

`[who you are] + [what you do] + [what you write about or help with]`

For example:

I am a writer and strategist publishing guides about personal websites, site structure, and organic growth for small independent projects.

That is short, specific, and useful.

## Example 1: The writer bio

I am a writer focused on personal websites, editorial systems, and how small sites grow through structure rather than noise.

Why it works:

- it gives a domain
- it feels coherent
- it is easy to reuse on article pages

## Example 2: The consultant bio

I help small teams clarify their websites, content systems, and information architecture, and I write here about the patterns that work.

Why it works:

- it combines professional identity and site purpose
- it implies experience without sounding inflated
- it points naturally toward the writing

## Example 3: The designer or developer bio

I design and build websites, then write about the structure, copy, and decisions that make them easier to understand and easier to grow.

Why it works:

- it links the practice and the publishing
- it feels concrete
- it works well on a hybrid portfolio-and-blog site

## Example 4: The generalist bio

I write about small systems that compound: websites, content libraries, and structures that stay useful over time.

Why it works:

- it gives personality
- it still defines the territory
- it avoids empty "multi-hyphenate" language

## Example 5: The credibility-first bio

I have spent the last decade working across content, design, and site strategy for small digital projects, and this site collects the practical lessons from that work.

Why it works:

- it includes proof
- it stays relevant to the site
- it can work under articles or in an about-page intro

## What makes a weak author bio

Weak bios often rely on:

- vague adjectives
- broad claims
- personality without context

Examples:

- passionate storyteller and lifelong learner
- helping brands thrive through innovation
- creative thinker building at the intersection of everything

These are not memorable because they do not say enough.

## Keep it short enough to reuse

The best short bio is usually one or two sentences.

That keeps it flexible enough to use in:

- article bylines
- newsletter intros
- sidebar cards
- contact sections

If the bio becomes too long, it stops functioning as a short bio and starts becoming a compressed about page.

## Match the bio to the page

You can adjust the same base bio depending on placement.

For example:

- article pages can emphasize what you write about
- work pages can emphasize what you do
- contact pages can emphasize what kinds of conversations are relevant

That is more effective than trying to make one universal paragraph do every job.
