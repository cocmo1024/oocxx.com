A contact page seems simple, but it often reveals how serious a website really is.

Weak contact pages create hesitation.

They make visitors wonder:

- Is this site active?
- Is there a real person behind it?
- What kind of message is appropriate?
- Will anyone reply?

A good contact page removes that uncertainty quickly.

## What the contact page needs to do

The page should answer four things:

- how to contact you
- what types of inquiries make sense
- what the visitor should expect after reaching out
- what to do if they only want to keep reading

That means the page is not only a utility page. It is also a trust page.

## The simplest contact page structure

For most personal websites, this structure is enough:

1. a short introduction
2. one clear contact method
3. a sentence on what people can contact you about
4. a short expectation on response time
5. links to important pages if contact is not the next step

That is clean, readable, and practical.

## Lead with a clear invitation

The first lines should explain what kinds of messages are welcome.

For example:

- questions about your writing
- project inquiries
- speaking requests
- thoughtful replies from readers

This helps people self-sort and reduces vague outreach.

It also makes the page feel more intentional than a lonely email address floating on the screen.

## Use one primary contact method

Most personal websites do not need five contact options.

Choose one primary method:

- email
- a contact form
- a clearly preferred social or messaging channel

If email is the main option, show it clearly.

If you use a form, keep it short.

Too many options make the page feel messy and increase friction. A small site usually benefits more from clarity than from redundancy.

## Say what people should expect

One short expectation line helps a lot.

For example:

- I usually reply within three business days.
- If your note is about the site or a recent guide, I am more likely to answer by email.
- For project inquiries, include budget and timeline.

This filters low-quality messages without sounding hostile.

## Add context, not fluff

You do not need to turn the contact page into another about page.

But a little context helps:

- what you work on
- why someone might contact you
- whether you are open to collaborations, freelance work, or interviews

If the site already has a strong [about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/), keep the contact page short and link back to that page when needed.

## Common contact page mistakes

The most common mistakes are:

- no actual contact method
- vague copy such as "feel free to reach out anytime"
- too many channels with no clear preference
- zero indication of what the site owner wants to hear about
- no response expectations

These mistakes do not only hurt usability. They make the site feel less trustworthy.

## Contact pages should connect to the rest of the site

The contact page should not feel disconnected from the site structure.

It should usually link to:

- the homepage
- the about page
- the main writing or work section

That is one reason the overall page system matters. If you have not planned the page structure yet, read [what pages should a personal website have](/blog/what-pages-should-a-personal-website-have/).

## A lightweight contact page template

If you want a starting point, use this:

### Contact

If you want to get in touch about my writing, a project, or a thoughtful question related to the site, email me at `hello@example.com`.

I usually reply within a few business days.

If you are new here, you may want to start with the [homepage](/) or browse the [guides](/blog/).

That is enough.

## Keep the page low-friction

The best contact page is simple enough that a real person can act on it immediately.

They should not have to decode your tone, search for the email address, or wonder whether you actually want to hear from them.

A contact page is a small page, but it has an outsized effect on trust.
