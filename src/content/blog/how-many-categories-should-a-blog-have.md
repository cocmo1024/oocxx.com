Most small blogs should have fewer categories than they currently do.

That is the short answer.

For many independent sites, four to eight categories is enough.

## Why category count matters

Categories are not decorative labels.

They shape:

- archive structure
- navigation clarity
- how easily readers understand the site
- how clean or noisy your crawl paths become

Too many categories make the site feel fragmented. Too few can make everything collapse into one vague bucket.

## A practical range for small blogs

For many small blogs, a good range is:

- 4 to 8 categories

That is enough to create clear sections without turning the site into a directory of tiny shelves.

If the blog is still small, even 3 to 5 categories can be enough.

## Why most blogs overdo categories

Blogs usually end up with too many categories because people:

- create a new category for every interesting subtopic
- confuse categories with tags
- build taxonomy around what exists this week instead of what the site should become

The result is a structure that keeps expanding while becoming less useful.

## What categories should do

A category should represent a broad, stable part of the site.

It should help answer:

- what major subject does this post belong to?

That means categories should be:

- broad enough to hold several posts
- stable enough to last
- clear enough for a reader to understand quickly

If you need the broader distinction, [blog categories vs tags](/blog/blog-categories-vs-tags/) covers that system in full.

## When you probably have too many categories

You probably have too many when:

- several categories contain only one or two posts
- the names overlap heavily
- the main navigation keeps expanding
- you are unsure where new posts belong

These are all signs that the structure is too granular.

## When you may need more categories

You may need more categories when:

- one category has become too broad to browse well
- the site genuinely covers a new major topic
- readers would benefit from a clearer separation between two large bodies of content

The key word is major.

Do not add a category for a minor variation that could live as a tag instead.

## Categories should match the site you want later

Do not only design categories for the current archive.

Design them for the next twenty or fifty posts.

That is why category planning fits closely with [website navigation best practices](/blog/website-navigation-best-practices/) and [how to build an editorial calendar for a small blog](/blog/editorial-calendar-for-small-blog/).

## A simple way to review your category count

Ask:

- does each category describe a real major section of the site?
- can each category plausibly hold multiple useful posts?
- would a reader understand the difference between them?

If the answer is no, merge or remove categories until the structure gets cleaner.

## What to do instead of adding another category

Before adding a new category, consider:

- using a tag
- using internal links
- creating a support post inside an existing category

Often the site needs stronger cross-linking, not another shelf.

## The goal is a legible archive

Category count matters because archive clarity matters.

A good category system should make the site feel:

- smaller than it is
- clearer than it was
- easier to browse as it grows

That is what makes the structure worth maintaining.
