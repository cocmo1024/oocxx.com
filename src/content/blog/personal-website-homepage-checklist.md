A homepage fails when it makes the visitor work too hard.

The page should not leave people asking:

- Who is this for?
- What does this person do?
- Is this a portfolio, a blog, or something else?
- Where should I click next?

The best homepage is not the most dramatic one. It is the one that creates orientation quickly.

If you want to see the broader site shapes that strong homepages support, start with these [personal website examples](/blog/personal-website-examples/).

## What a homepage needs to do

At minimum, a personal website homepage should:

- explain what the site is about
- give a short introduction to the person or project
- point toward the most important next pages
- show evidence that the site is active and useful

That is enough.

The homepage is not meant to contain everything. It is meant to route people intelligently.

## Above the fold: the first screen matters most

The first screen should usually include:

- a headline that says what you do or what the site is for
- a short supporting sentence
- a simple primary navigation
- one obvious next step

Weak headline:

Hi, I'm Alex.

Stronger headline:

Writer and strategist publishing practical guides for small websites.

The second version gives the visitor context immediately.

## Include proof without turning the page into a wall

After the first screen, add proof. That proof might be:

- selected projects
- recent writing
- clients or publications
- a short credibility line on the about section

Keep it selective. A homepage should show enough to build confidence, not so much that it turns into an archive dump.

## Make the navigation boring in the best way

Most personal websites need only a few top-level links:

- Home
- About
- Work or Projects
- Blog or Guides
- Contact

That is usually enough.

If the navigation is vague, visitors hesitate. If it is overloaded, they stall. Use [website navigation best practices](/blog/website-navigation-best-practices/) if your menu is starting to sprawl.

## Put useful writing on the homepage

If you want search traffic, your homepage should hint that the site contains useful articles, not just a biography.

Good homepage modules include:

- featured guide
- latest posts
- popular articles
- topic clusters

This turns the homepage into a routing layer for the rest of the site.

## Your about summary should be short

Do not paste your full life story onto the homepage.

Instead:

- give a two- or three-sentence summary
- explain what you care about
- link to the full about page

The homepage also does not need to carry all your contact details. A clean route to a dedicated [contact page](/blog/what-to-put-on-a-contact-page/) is usually better.

Then write the full version properly. This guide on [how to write an about page](/blog/how-to-write-an-about-page/) will help.

## Common homepage mistakes

The most common mistakes are:

- a headline that says nothing
- no clear indication of who the site is for
- too many modules before any real explanation
- oversized hero sections with weak copy
- no featured writing or proof
- confusing navigation labels

Most of these problems are structural, not visual.

## A simple homepage template

If you want a clean default structure, use this:

1. Headline and one-sentence positioning statement
2. Main navigation
3. Featured work or featured guide
4. Short about section
5. Recent writing or key resources
6. Footer with contact and secondary links

That pattern works because it answers the main visitor questions in order.

## The test that matters

Open your homepage and ask:

- Can a stranger understand the site in ten seconds?
- Can they tell what I make or write about?
- Can they find the next useful page without guessing?

If the answer is no, the homepage needs work.
